Things to know before we begin

A quick overview of key security concepts.

The CIA Triad

The Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA) Triad are the three main goals of information security. They are a model for the development of security policies.

Confidentiality

Confidentiality protects information from unauthorized disclosure. This is about keeping sensitive data private.

Key Concepts:

  • PII (Personally Identifiable Information): Any data that can identify an individual.
  • PHI (Protected Health Information): Healthcare records regulated by HIPAA.

Threats to Confidentiality

  • Snooping: Mitigated by "clean desk policies."
  • Dumpster Diving: Mitigated by shredding documents.
  • Eavesdropping: Mitigated by rules for sensitive conversations.
  • Wiretapping: Mitigated by encryption.
  • Social Engineering: Mitigated by "educating users."

Integrity

Integrity protects information from unauthorized changes. This ensures data is accurate and trustworthy.

Threats to Integrity

  • Unauthorized Modification: Mitigated by the least privilege principle.
  • Impersonation: Mitigated by "user education."
  • Man-in-the-Middle (MITM): Mitigated by encryption.
  • Replay Attacks: Mitigated by encryption.

Availability

Availability protects authorized access to systems and data. This ensures users can get to what they need, when they need it.

Threats to Availability

  • Denial of Service (DoS): Mitigated by blocking unauthorized connections.
  • Power Outages: Mitigated with redundant power and generators.
  • Hardware Failures: Mitigated with redundant components.
  • Destruction: Mitigated with data backups and disaster recovery plans.

Authentication and Authorization (AAA)

  1. Identification: Making a claim of identity (e.g., a username).
  2. Authentication: Proving that claim of identity (e.g., a password).
  3. Authorization: Ensuring that an action is allowed based on your identity.
  4. Accounting: Tracking and logging user activity.

Password Security

Strong passwords are a key part of authentication. Mechanisms include:

  • Length: Minimum number of characters.
  • Complexity: Required character types (e.g., symbols, numbers).
  • Expiration: Forcing password changes periodically.
  • History: Preventing password reuse.

Multi-factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA combines at least two different authentication factors to verify identity.

  • Something you know: Passwords, PINs, security questions.
  • Something you are: Biometric security (fingerprint, face).
  • Something you have: Software or hardware tokens.

Single Sign-On (SSO)

SSO allows users to log in once and gain access to multiple systems without re-authenticating. This improves user experience and security.

Non-repudiation

Non-repudiation prevents someone from falsely denying they performed a particular action.

Examples include:

  • Signed contracts
  • Digital signatures
  • Video surveillance

Privacy

Privacy is the right of an individual to control the distribution of information about themselves.

Key concerns include:

  • Protecting our own data.
  • Educating users about their data.
  • Protecting data collected by organizations.
