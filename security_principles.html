L1: Security Principles

Module 1: Understand the Security Concepts of Information Assurance

Domains D1.1.1 - D1.1.6

Confidentiality

It relates to permitting authorized access to information, while protecting it from improper disclosure.

Key Concepts:

  • PII (Personally Identifiable Information): Data used to identify an individual.
  • PHI (Protected Health Information): Information about one's health status.
  • Sensitive Information: Data whose improper disclosure would harm an organization or individual.

Threats to Confidentiality

  • Snooping: Gathering information left out in the open. Mitigated by clean desk policies.
  • Dumpster Diving: Looking for sensitive materials in the trash. Mitigated by paper shredding.
  • Eavesdropping: Secretly listening to a conversation. Mitigated by rules for sensitive conversations.
  • Wiretapping: The electronic version of eavesdropping. Mitigated by using encryption.
  • Social Engineering: Tricking users into revealing information. Mitigated by user education.

Integrity

Ensuring data is complete, accurate, and consistent. It applies to data in storage, during processing, and in transit.

Key Concepts:

  • Consistency: All instances of data are identical.
  • Baseline: A documented, secure configuration of a system or data at a certain point.

Threats to Integrity

  • Unauthorized Modification: Making changes without permission. Mitigated by least privilege principle.
  • Impersonation: Pretending to be someone else. Mitigated by user education.
  • Man-In-The-Middle (MITM): An attacker intercepts and monitors a communication session.
  • Replay Attacks: Eavesdropping on logins and reusing captured credentials.

For MiTM and Replay attacks, the best defense is encryption.

Availability

Timely and reliable access to data and systems for authorized users when and where it is needed.

Key Concepts:

  • Criticality: A measure of how much an organization depends on a system or data for its mission.

Not all systems need to be available 100% of the time, but critical systems must meet business requirements.

Threats to Availability

  • Denial of Service (DoS): Can be mitigated with firewalls.
  • Power Outages: Can be mitigated with redundant power and generators.
  • Hardware Failures: Can be mitigated with redundant components.
  • Destruction: Can be mitigated with backups.

The Triple A (AAA)

The three steps to gain access to a system.

  • Identification: Making a claim of identity.
  • Authentication: Validating that the user is the rightful owner of that identity.
  • Authorization: Ensuring that an action is allowed.
  • Accounting: Maintaining logs of activity.

Authentication Methods

  • Something You Know: Passwords or passphrases.
  • Something You Have: Tokens, memory cards, or smart cards.
  • Something You Are: Biometrics (fingerprints, face).

Single-Factor Authentication (SFA) uses only one of these methods. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) uses two or more.

Password Best Practices

  • Password length and complexity requirements.
  • Password history to prevent reuse.
  • Encourage users not to reuse passwords across multiple sites.
  • Provide a quick and easy way to change passwords.
  • Password managers facilitate strong, unique passwords.

Non-repudiation

Legal term defined as the protection against an individual falsely denying having performed a particular action.

Privacy

The right of an individual to control the distribution of information about themselves.

Legislation like GDPR can have a global impact on how companies handle personal data.

Base Concepts Review

  • Authentication: Validating an identity claim.
  • Integrity: Data has not been altered in an unauthorized manner.
  • Confidentiality: Data is not disclosed to unauthorized persons.
  • Privacy: The right to control personal information.
  • Availability: Timely and reliable access to information.
  • Non-repudiation: The inability to deny taking an action.
  • Authorization: The permission to access a resource.

Module 2: Risk Management Process

Identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks.

Domains D1.2.1 - D1.2.2

What is Risk?

A measure of the extent to which an entity is threatened by a potential circumstance or event. It is a combination of the adverse impacts that would arise and the likelihood of its occurrence.

The Risk Matrix:

  • Low Probability, Low Impact
  • Low Probability, High Impact
  • High Probability, Low Impact
  • High Probability, High Impact

Risk Management Terminology

  • Asset: Something of value that needs protection.
  • Vulnerability: A gap or weakness in an organization's protection.
  • Threat: A person or entity that aims to exploit a vulnerability.
  • Likelihood: The probability that a vulnerability will be exploited.
  • Impact: The magnitude of harm that can be expected from an event.

Risk Identification & Assessment

Risk identification is a continuous process. Everyone in the organization is responsible for it.

Risk assessment is the process of identifying, estimating, and prioritizing risks. It results in a report for management to determine mitigation priorities.

Risk Treatment Options

  • Avoidance: Eliminating the risk entirely by ceasing the activity.
  • Acceptance: Taking no action to reduce the risk.
  • Mitigation: Taking actions to prevent or reduce the possibility of a risk event.
  • Transfer: Passing the risk to another party (e.g., insurance).

Risk Priorities & Tolerance

Organizations use a risk matrix to prioritize risks based on likelihood and impact.

Risk Tolerance is the level of risk an organization is willing to accept. This varies by company and even by department.

Module 3: Security Controls

The safeguards and countermeasures that protect systems.

Domains D1.3.1 - D1.3.3

Types of Controls

  • Physical: Hardware, architecture, or actions that control the physical environment (e.g., badge readers, door locks).
  • Technical (Logical): Implemented by computer systems and networks (e.g., access control lists).
  • Administrative (Managerial): Directives and guidelines for people (e.g., acceptable use policies, training).
