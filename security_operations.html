L5 Security Operations

Module 1: Understand Data Security

Hardening is the process of applying secure configurations to reduce the attack surface and locking down various hardware, communications systems and software.

Data Handling

Data itself goes through its own life cycle as users create, use, share and modify it. The data security life cycle model is useful because it can align easily with the different roles that people and organizations perform.

Six Major Sets of Activities:

  1. Creating the knowledge
  2. Storing or recording it
  3. Using the knowledge
  4. Sharing the data
  5. Archiving the data
  6. Destroying the data

Data Handling Practices

Classification

Classification dictates rules and restrictions about how that information can be used, stored or shared with others. It is the process of recognizing the organizational impacts if the information suffers any security compromises related to its confidentiality, integrity and availability.

Data Handling Practices

Labeling

Security labels are part of implementing controls to protect classified information. It is a simple way of assigning a level of sensitivity to a data asset, such that the higher the level, the greater the presumed harm to the organization, and thus the greater security protection the data asset requires.

Data Sensitivity Levels

  • Highly restricted: Compromise could put the organization’s future existence at risk.

  • Moderately restricted: Compromise could lead to loss of temporary competitive advantage or revenue.

  • Low sensitivity: Compromise could cause minor disruptions, delays or impacts.

  • Unrestricted public data: No harm can come from further dissemination.

Data Handling Practices

Retention

Information and data should be kept only for as long as it is beneficial. Data retention policies are applicable both for hard copies and for electronic data.

Security professionals should ensure that data destruction is performed when an asset has reached its retention limit.

Data Handling Practices

Destruction

Data that might be left on media after deleting is known as **remanence** and may be a significant security concern.

  • Clearing: writing multiple patterns of random values throughout all storage media.

  • Purging: eliminating the chance that residual physical effects may still be recovered.

  • Physical destruction: the ultimate remedy to data remanence.

Logging & Monitoring

Logging is the primary form of instrumentation that attempts to capture signals generated by events. **Logging imposes a computational cost but is invaluable when determining accountability**.

Logging and monitoring the health of the information environment is essential to identifying inefficient or improperly performing systems and detecting compromises.

Event Logging Best Practices

Ingress monitoring:

Refers to surveillance and assessment of all inbound communications traffic and access attempts.

Egress monitoring:

Used to regulate data leaving the organization’s IT environment. The term currently used in conjunction with this effort is **data loss prevention (DLP)**.

Encryption Overview

Cryptography is used to protect information by keeping its meaning or content secret and making it unintelligible to someone who does not have a way to decrypt that protected information.

Properly used, cryptographic solutions provide services that help achieve required systems security postures, including:

  • Confidentiality: Hiding or obscuring a message so that it cannot be understood by anyone except the intended recipient.

  • Integrity: Hash functions and digital signatures can provide integrity services that allow a recipient to verify that a message has not been altered.

Module 2: Understand System Hardening

Configuration management is a process and discipline used to ensure that the only changes made to a system are those that have been authorized and validated.

It is both a decision-making process and a set of control processes.

Configuration Management

  1. Identification: Baseline identification of a system and all its components, interfaces and documentation.

  2. Baseline: A security baseline is a minimum level of protection that can be used as a reference point.

  3. Change Control: An update process for requesting changes to a baseline.

  4. Verification & Audit: A regression and validation process to verify that nothing in the system was broken by a newly applied set of changes.

Inventory & Baselines

Making an inventory of all the information assets is the first step in any asset management process. **You can’t protect what you don’t know you have.**

The **baseline** is a total inventory of all the system’s components, hardware, software, data, administrative controls, documentation and user instructions. All further comparisons and development are measured against the baselines.

Updates & Patches

Updates must be acceptance tested and regression tested to verify that the modifications did not introduce other erroneous or unexpected behaviors.

The challenge for the security professional is maintaining all patches. An organization should **test the patch before rolling it out** across the organization and have a **rollback plan** in case it does not work.

Module 3: Best Practice Security Policies

An organization’s security policies define what “security” means to that organization. They express or impose behavioral or other constraints on the system and its use.

Common Security Policies

  • Data Handling Policy: Defines whether data is for internal use, restricted use, or can be made public.

  • Password Policy: Defines expectations of systems and users regarding password complexity, length, and change frequency.

Common Security Policies (Cont.)

  • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Defines acceptable use of the organization’s network and computer systems. It helps protect the organization from legal action.

  • Bring Your Own Device (BYOD): An organization may allow workers to use personally owned equipment for business use. This presents additional challenges for security professionals.

Common Security Policies (Cont.)

Privacy Policy

It is imperative that the organization documents that personnel understand and acknowledge the organization’s policies and procedures for handling personally identifiable information (PII).

This type of policy should stipulate which information is considered PII/ePHI, the appropriate handling procedures, and any enforcement mechanisms.

Common Security Policies (Cont.)

Change Management Policy

Change management is the discipline of transitioning from the current state to a future state. It consists of three major activities: deciding to change, making the change, and confirming that the change has been correctly accomplished.

Change management requires a process to implement the necessary changes so they do not adversely affect business operations.

Change Management Components

  • Documentation: All major change management practices start with a request for change (RFC).

  • Approval: Processes for evaluating RFCs, stakeholder reviews, and approvals or rejections.

  • Rollback: A variety of activities to be completed in case a change is not successful, defined in a rollback plan.

Module 4: Security Awareness Training

The purpose of awareness training is to make sure everyone knows what is expected of them, based on responsibilities and accountabilities.

It is crucial that the organization informs its employees and staff how to recognize security problems and how to operate in a secure manner.

Three Types of Learning

  • Education: Helps learners improve their understanding of ideas and their ability to relate and apply them.

  • Training: Focuses on building proficiency in a specific set of skills or actions.

  • Awareness: Activities that attract and engage the learner’s attention by acquainting them with aspects of an issue or concern.

Training Examples

Fire Safety

  • Education: A worker in a server room understands how fire detectors and suppression systems interact.

  • Training: The worker receives task-specific learning on proper actions to take during an alarm.

  • Awareness: Appropriate signage and markings are posted to help workers detect an anomaly and respond to an alarm.

Training Examples (Cont.)

Anti-Phishing Campaign

  • Education: Helps select groups of users better understand how social engineering attacks are conducted.

  • Training: Helps users increase their proficiency in recognizing potential phishing attempts.

  • Awareness: Raises users’ overall awareness of the threat posed by phishing

Social Engineering Tactics

Social engineering is an important part of any security awareness training program for one very simple reason: **bad actors know that it works**.

For cyberattackers, social engineering is an inexpensive investment with a potentially very high payoff.

Vishing (Phone Phishing)

Using a rogue interactive voice response (IVR) system to re-create a legitimate-sounding copy of a bank or other institution’s IVR system.

The victim is prompted to call in to the "bank" to verify information such as account numbers, PIN, or answers to security questions.

Pretexting & Quid Pro Quo

  • Pretexting: The human equivalent of phishing, where someone impersonates an authority figure or a trusted individual to gain access to login information.

  • Quid pro quo: A request for your password or login credentials in exchange for some compensation, such as a "free gift" or monetary payment.

Tailgating

The practice of following an authorized user into a restricted area or system.

In a more sophisticated version, someone may ask to borrow your phone or laptop to perform a simple action when they are actually installing malicious software onto your device.

Password Protection

Many password managers will store a user’s passwords, but the greatest disadvantage is the risk of compromise of the password manager itself.

Organizations should encourage the use of different passwords for different systems and provide a recommended password management solution.

Avoid These Password Practices

  • Reusing passwords for multiple systems, especially for business and personal use.

  • Writing down passwords and leaving them in unsecured areas.

  • Sharing a password with tech support or a co-worker.
