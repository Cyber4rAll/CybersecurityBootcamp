Incident Response, Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery

Planning for the Unexpected

When we're talking about IR, BC, and DR, we're focusing on availability, achieved through these disciplines.

  • Incident Response (IR) plan responds to unexpected changes in operating conditions to keep the business operating.
  • Business Continuity (BC) plan enables the business to continue operating throughout the crisis.
  • Disaster Recovery (DR) plan restores normal operations as quickly as possible if IR and BC are insufficient.

Module 1: Understand Incident Response

Key terminology and goals; relationship to BCM.

Incident Terminology (1/2)

Breach (NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5): The loss of control, compromise, unauthorized disclosure, unauthorized acquisition, or similar occurrence where a non-authorized person accesses (or potentially accesses) PII, or an authorized user accesses PII for an unauthorized purpose.

Event (NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 2): Any observable occurrence in a network or system.

Exploit: A particular attack that exploits system vulnerabilities.

Incident: An event that actually or potentially jeopardizes the confidentiality, integrity, or availability (CIA) of an information system or its information.

Incident Terminology (2/2)

Intrusion (IETF RFC 4949 v2): A deliberate security incident in which an intruder gains, or attempts to gain, access to a system/resource without authorization.

Threat (NIST SP 800-30 Rev. 1): Any circumstance or event with potential to adversely impact organizational operations, assets, individuals, or other organizations via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification, or DoS.

Vulnerability (NIST SP 800-30 Rev. 1): A weakness in systems, security procedures, or controls that could be exploited by a threat source.

Zero Day: A previously unknown vulnerability likely to be exploited without detection or prevention because it doesn't match known signatures or methods.

The Goal of Incident Response

The priority of any incident response is to protect life, health, and safety. When prioritizing, always choose safety first.

The primary goal is preparedness: a policy and response plan that lead the organization through the crisis. Some organizations call this crisis management.

An event is any measurable occurrence; most are harmless. If it can disrupt the business mission, it becomes an incident.

Every organization should maintain an incident response plan to preserve viability. IR planning is a subset of Business Continuity Management (BCM), focused on reducing impact and resuming operations quickly.

Incident Response Plan: Structure

The IR policy should reference a plan that all employees follow according to their role. The plan is a living representation of policy and may include multiple procedures and standards.

The organization's vision, strategy, and mission should shape the IR process. Procedures define the technical processes, techniques, checklists, and tools the teams use during response.

Clarity of roles and responsibilities plus documentation standards is critical for repeatability and auditability.

IR Plan Components: Preparation

  • Develop an IR policy approved by management.
  • Identify critical data/systems and single points of failure.
  • Train staff on IR; establish an incident response team.
  • Practice incident identification and define roles & responsibilities.
  • Plan stakeholder communications and assume primary channels may fail (define alternates).

IR Plan Components: Stages

  • Detection & Analysis: Monitor attack vectors; use intel; prioritize response; standardize incident documentation.
  • Containment, Eradication & Recovery: Gather evidence; identify attacker/TTPs; choose containment; isolate and remediate.
  • Post-incident Activity: Retain evidence; document lessons learned; retrospective to feed back into Preparation → Detection/Analysis → Containment/Recovery.

Incident Response Team (Who)

Alongside a Security Operations Center (SOC), establish a cross-functional Incident Response Team (IRT).

  • Representative(s) of senior management
  • Information security professionals
  • Legal representatives
  • Public affairs / communications
  • Engineering representatives (system & network)

Team members receive training on the IR plan and their roles.

Incident Response Team (Responsibilities)

  • Investigate incidents and assess damage.
  • Collect evidence and report the incident.
  • Initiate and oversee recovery procedures.
  • Participate in remediation, lessons learned, and root cause analysis.
  • Many orgs formalize as CIRT/CSIRT teams.

During an incident, the team must:

  • Determine the amount and scope of damage.
  • Determine if confidential information was compromised.
  • Implement required recovery procedures and restore security.
  • Supervise additional security measures to prevent recurrence.

Module 2: Understand Business Continuity (BC)

Purpose, components, and testing of the BCP.

The Importance of Business Continuity

A BCP sustains business operations while recovering from significant disruption.

Communication is key: multiple methods, backup numbers, and phone trees for redundancy.

Management involvement ensures timely authorizations (e.g., shutting down critical areas) and prioritization.

Keep critical supply chain contacts, law enforcement, and offsite contacts at hand. Example: US hospitals may receive priority access to resilient networks during severe cyber incidents to maintain essential activity.

Components of a Business Continuity Plan

BCP is the proactive development of procedures to restore business operations after a disaster or major disruption. Cross-functional participation is essential.

  • List of BCP team members with multiple contact methods and backups.
  • Immediate response procedures and safety checklists (security/safety, fire suppression, notifying emergency-response agencies).
  • Notification systems and call trees to alert personnel that the BCP is enacted.
  • Guidance for management, including designation of authority.
  • Clear triggers for when and how to enact the plan.
  • Contact numbers for critical supply chain partners (vendors, customers, external providers, third parties).
  • Technology alignment with business needs to protect confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

How Often Should You Test the BCP?

Test the BCP routinely. Define a cadence (e.g., quarterly, semi-annually, annually) and also test when significant business or IT changes occur.

Module 3: Understand Disaster Recovery (DR)

Goal of DR and core plan components.

The Goal of Disaster Recovery

DR planning steps in where BC leaves off. During disasters or major interruptions, the DRP guides actions until the business is restored to last-known reliable operations.

DR focuses on restoring IT and communications services during disruption and throughout restoration. Business functions might recover independently, but IT recovery is often crucial to sustaining operations.

Components of a DRP

  • Executive summary providing a high-level overview.
  • Department-specific plans for affected units.
  • Technical guides for IT personnel implementing/maintaining critical backup systems and alternate sites.
  • Full plan copies for critical DR team members.
  • Checklists tailored to roles:
    • Critical DR team members — concise action guides in chaotic conditions.
    • IT personnel — technical runbooks to bring alternate sites up.
    • Managers & PR — high-level comms guides to inform stakeholders while freeing technical staff.

DRP Governance & Roles

  • Executive management should approve the plan and receive a high-level summary.
  • Public Relations should be included to manage communications with all stakeholders.
  • IT personnel are primarily responsible for the disaster recovery team.
